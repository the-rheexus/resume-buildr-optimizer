
# Resume Standards (2026)
 
Authoritative reference for resume format, structure, content, and design for recent MBA graduates targeting strategy, product management, or analytics roles in the US market.
 
---
 
## 1. Top-line standards
 
A standout MBA resume in 2026 has these qualities:
 
1. **One page, single column.** Even with eight to twelve years of pre-MBA experience.
2. **Business-document feel.** Dense but not cramped. Clear hierarchy. Generous white space at section breaks. No graphics, photos, icons, tables, text boxes, or sidebars.
3. **Outcomes-first bullets.** Every bullet answers "so what." Quantified scope, scale, or impact in at least 70 percent of bullets.
4. **Leadership and structured problem solving surfaced explicitly.** Not just task lists. Show cross-functional influence, ownership, and progression.
5. **Tight alignment to a target recruiting lane.** Bullets, keywords, and ordering oriented to the post-MBA role.
6. **Optimized for both human and machine reading.** Standard headings, parseable formatting, keywords drawn from target job descriptions and embedded in context (not in a dump).
---
 
## 2. Standard section order
 
For on-campus and post-MBA recruiting:
 
1. Header
2. Education
3. Professional Experience
4. Leadership and Activities (sometimes "Leadership and Involvement")
5. Skills and Interests
Standard headings only. Recognizable to both humans and parsers. Do not rename to "What I've Done" or "My Journey."
 
### 2.1 Header
- Name in bold, slightly larger font (12 to 14 pt)
- Single line of contact: city and state, mobile, professional email, LinkedIn URL
- No street address. No photo. No date of birth, marital status, nationality, or other personal details. These create bias risk and waste space.
- LinkedIn URL should be customized (`linkedin.com/in/firstnamelastname`), not the default with random digits.
### 2.2 Education
- MBA program first: school, degree, location, graduation year
- Concentration if relevant to target lane (for example, "Concentration: Strategy and Analytics")
- GPA only if 3.5+ and the program does not prohibit disclosure
- GMAT or GRE only if strong (GMAT 720+, GRE 320+) and the candidate is fewer than three years out from the test
- Relevant honors, awards, scholarships, and leadership roles as one to three bullets
- Undergraduate institution next: school, degree, graduation year, GPA if 3.5+, honors if any
### 2.3 Professional Experience
- Reverse chronological. Pre-MBA roles, MBA summer internship (once completed), and any MBA-period work
- Each role on a single line: title, company, location, dates
- Two to six bullets per role. More bullets for the most recent or most relevant role; fewer for older or less relevant roles.
- Promotions called out explicitly. Example: "Promoted from Analyst to Senior Analyst in 14 months."
- Use past tense for completed roles, present tense only for the current ongoing role.
### 2.4 Leadership and Activities
- Campus leadership, clubs, case competitions, volunteer roles, board positions, startup or side projects
- Same bullet structure as Professional Experience: action plus outcome
- Include for current MBA cohort only when the activity demonstrates leadership, ownership, or skills relevant to the target lane
### 2.5 Skills and Interests
- Compact, grouped into two to four categories. See section 5 below.
- One short line of personal interests at the end. Humanizes the profile; supports networking. Avoid generic ("traveling, music"); pick specific ("competitive chess, vintage cocktail history, electronic music production").

## 3. Formatting specifications
 
| Element | Standard |
|---|---|
| File format for submission | PDF generated from a text-based source. .docx as backup if the application explicitly requests it. |
| Font | Calibri, Arial, Helvetica, Garamond, or Times New Roman. Body 10 to 11 pt; headings 11 to 12 pt. |
| Margins | 0.5 to 0.75 inch on all sides |
| Color | Black text on white background. A single accent color (dark blue or dark gray) for section headings is acceptable but not required. |
| Bullets | Standard round or simple dash bullets. Single style throughout. |
| Date format | Consistent across all entries: either "Aug 2023 to May 2026" or "08/2023 to 05/2026". Pick one and apply uniformly. |
| Punctuation | Bullets either all end in periods or none do. Pick one. |
| Number format | "$3.5M" or "$3.5 million", not both. "27%" not "27 percent" within bullets. |
| Headings style | All caps or small caps; bold; slightly larger than body |
| Line spacing | 1.0 to 1.15 within sections; slightly more between sections |
| Forbidden | Photos, icons, logos, infographics, charts, columns, tables, text boxes, ratings ("Python four out of five stars"), color shading, headers and footers containing critical content |
 
### 3.1 Why no tables, columns, or graphics
ATS and AI screeners parse left-to-right, top-to-bottom. Multi-column layouts can scramble reading order. Graphics and images are not parsed at all. Information inside text boxes may be skipped entirely by some systems. Headers and footers are inconsistently parsed. Keep all critical content in the main body, single column.

## 4. Bullet writing
 
### 4.1 The standard structure
 
> **Strong action verb + context or problem + what you did + business result with metric**
 
Average bullet:
> Responsible for managing the West region sales team.
 
Strong bullet:
> Led 8-person West region sales team, redesigning territory coverage and incentive plans; increased quarterly revenue 27% and cut customer churn 8% within two quarters.
 
### 4.2 Action verbs
 
Use a different verb for every bullet within a role. Stronger verbs imply ownership and outcomes. Cycle through categories.
 
**Leadership and ownership:** Led, Owned, Spearheaded, Drove, Championed, Directed, Built, Founded, Launched, Scaled
**Analysis and strategy:** Analyzed, Modeled, Synthesized, Diagnosed, Evaluated, Forecasted, Benchmarked, Structured, Sized, Quantified
**Execution and operations:** Executed, Delivered, Implemented, Deployed, Optimized, Streamlined, Automated, Standardized, Reengineered
**Influence and communication:** Negotiated, Persuaded, Aligned, Influenced, Briefed, Presented, Recommended, Championed, Mobilized
**Building and creating:** Built, Designed, Developed, Created, Authored, Architected, Prototyped
 
Avoid weak verbs: Helped, Assisted, Worked on, Participated in, Was responsible for, Supported.
 
### 4.3 Quantification
 
Target at least 70 percent of bullets containing a number or specific scale. Dimensions:
 
- **Financial:** Revenue, margin, cost reduction, budget owned, deal size, ARR, GMV
- **Operational:** Cycle time, throughput, error rate, process steps eliminated
- **Customer and growth:** NPS, CSAT, churn, retention, adoption, conversion, MAUs, DAUs
- **People and leadership:** Team size, stakeholders, cross-functional groups led, training participants
- **Scale:** Geographic regions, customers, units, countries, portfolios, products
If exact numbers are confidential, use percentages, ranges, or scale descriptors. Every bullet should suggest scope.
 
### 4.4 What to cut
 
- Vague adjectives without numbers: "significant," "substantial," "important," "large," "successful"
- Buzzwords without evidence: "innovative," "results-oriented," "team player," "strategic thinker"
- Sentence-level redundancy: "Responsible for being accountable for"
- Tasks without outcomes: "Created weekly reports" (rewrite to show what the reports changed)
- Skills lists masquerading as bullets: "Used Excel, SQL, Python, and Tableau" (move to Skills section; show one tool in a bullet with a specific outcome)
### 4.5 Bullet length
 
Target one to two lines per bullet. No bullet should exceed two lines. If a bullet runs to three lines, the content is either too dense or contains a fact that should be split off or cut.

## 5. Skills section
 
### 5.1 Structure
Group skills into two to four named categories. Examples:
 
> **Analytics and Tools:** SQL (advanced), Python (intermediate), Tableau, Looker, Excel (advanced), dbt
> **Strategy and Finance:** Financial modeling, market sizing, competitive analysis, pricing strategy, valuation
> **Product and Process:** A/B testing, experimentation design, product discovery, Jira, Figma, Agile
> **Languages:** Spanish (professional), French (conversational)
 
### 5.2 What belongs in Skills
 
- Hard skills directly relevant to the target lane (see `target_lane_rubrics_and_bullets.md`)
- Specific tools and software the candidate can use under interview pressure
- Languages with honest proficiency descriptors (native, fluent, professional, conversational, basic)
- Certifications relevant to the target lane (PMP, CFA Level X, AWS Cloud Practitioner, etc.)
### 5.3 What does not belong
 
- Soft skills as standalone tags: "leadership," "teamwork," "communication." These belong in experience bullets as evidence.
- Tools listed without supporting evidence elsewhere in the resume. Inflates ATS scoring but fails human screen.
- Star ratings, bar charts, or skill graphs.
- Generic technologies everyone knows: "Microsoft Word," "Email," "PowerPoint" (unless you can defend "advanced PowerPoint" with a specific deliverable; otherwise omit).
### 5.4 AI and data literacy
 
In 2026, mentioning AI tool use is no longer differentiating on its own. What matters is **specific tool plus specific outcome**. Strong examples:
 
> Built monthly executive reporting workflow combining SQL, Python, and Claude API; cut analyst preparation time from 12 hours to 90 minutes per cycle.
 
> Designed prompt-engineered classification pipeline (Claude, GPT-4) for support ticket triage; routed 84% of tickets correctly versus 61% baseline.
 
Weak example to avoid:
 
> Familiar with ChatGPT and other AI tools.

## 6. The pivot or career changer
 
For candidates pivoting industries or functions, the resume must make the pivot look credible without hiding the past.
 
### 6.1 Optional one-line headline
 
Just below the header, a single line can frame the pivot. Examples:
 
> Former aerospace engineer pivoting to B2B SaaS product management; combines six years of technical leadership with data-driven customer insight.
 
> Pre-MBA marketing analyst targeting corporate strategy roles in media and entertainment; built P&L narratives behind $40M revenue decisions.
 
Keep it under twenty-five words. Specific verbs and numbers. No filler.
 
### 6.2 Reordering bullets within a role
The most recent role first by date, but within each role, the bullets most relevant to the target lane go first. A marketing manager pivoting to PM puts customer research and cross-functional product collaboration bullets before campaign execution bullets.
 
### 6.3 Translating titles
Do not invent a fake title. The title remains what the company gave you. But the bullets translate the substance: "Marketing Manager" can credibly write bullets about product strategy, customer segmentation, and cross-functional leadership if those describe real work.

## 7. The recruiter scan
 
In practice, recruiters spend six to thirty seconds on the first scan. They look for:
 
1. **Brand and trajectory.** Recognizable employers or roles; visible progression (promotions, scope expansion).
2. **Evidence they can do the target job.** A few bullets that read like the target job's responsibilities, in the target lane's language.
3. **Leadership and ownership.** Leading teams or initiatives, not just contributing.
4. **Quantified, business-centric achievements.**
5. **Communication clarity.** No typos. Clean grammar. Structured bullets. Proxy for professional communication.
What gets cut in the first scan:
- Typos and inconsistent formatting (signals carelessness)
- Vague bullets that describe tasks without outcomes
- Mismatched lane (resume reads as if applying for a different function)
- One-page rule violations or cramped layouts
- Buzzword soup

## 8. The 2026 checklist
 
Before submitting any resume:
 
**Structure and format**
- [ ] Exactly one page
- [ ] Single column
- [ ] Standard sections in standard order
- [ ] Consistent dates, punctuation, capitalization
- [ ] PDF generated from a clean text source
- [ ] All critical content in the main body (nothing in headers, footers, text boxes)
**Content**
- [ ] Every bullet starts with a strong action verb
- [ ] At least 70 percent of bullets contain a number or specific scale
- [ ] Promotions and expanding scope are explicit
- [ ] No vague adjectives or buzzwords standing alone
- [ ] Past tense for completed roles; present only for current ongoing role
- [ ] No bullet exceeds two lines
**Target lane alignment**
- [ ] If a stranger read only the resume, they could identify the target lane
- [ ] Most lane-relevant bullets are placed first within each role
- [ ] Skills section matches lane expectations (see `target_lane_rubrics_and_bullets.md`)
- [ ] Three to five exact keywords from target job descriptions appear in context
**ATS and AI screening**
- [ ] Standard section headings used
- [ ] No graphics, tables, columns, text boxes
- [ ] No skills listed that cannot be demonstrated in an interview
- [ ] Resume passes a plain-text paste test (copy into a text editor; reads in correct order)
**Final review**
- [ ] Read aloud once. Any clunky sentence rewritten.
- [ ] Spell-check plus manual review. One typo can disqualify.
- [ ] One peer reviewer with no shared context flagged anything confusing.
