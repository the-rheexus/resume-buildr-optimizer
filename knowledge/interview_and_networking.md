# Interview Stories, LinkedIn About, and Networking
 
Frameworks for translating resume bullets into interview content, building a LinkedIn About section that reinforces the resume, and structuring networking outreach.
 
---
 
## 1. From resume to interview story
 
A resume bullet is roughly 15 to 30 words. An interview story is roughly 90 seconds spoken (about 200 to 250 words). Every interview-eligible bullet should have a corresponding story that the candidate can deliver fluently.
 
### 1.1 The CARL framework
 
Use CARL (Context, Action, Result, Learning) for behavioral interview stories. It is a tighter variant of STAR.
 
- **Context (15 to 25 seconds):** The situation. Business problem, stakes, your role.
- **Action (40 to 50 seconds):** What you did specifically. Use "I" not "we" unless team distinction is essential. Three to five concrete actions, with the structured logic behind them.
- **Result (15 to 20 seconds):** Quantified outcome. Match the metrics on the resume bullet.
- **Learning (10 to 15 seconds, optional):** What you would do differently or what the experience taught you. Used selectively; not every story needs it.
### 1.2 The story bank
 
Build a story bank of 8 to 12 stories covering the common interview prompt categories. Map each story to multiple prompts.
 
**Standard prompt categories to cover:**
 
1. Tell me about yourself / walk me through your resume
2. Why this role, why this company, why now
3. Leadership without authority
4. Cross-functional conflict or disagreement
5. Failure or mistake
6. Ambiguous problem with no clear answer
7. Working under tight deadline or resource constraint
8. Influencing a senior stakeholder
9. Data-driven decision (especially for PM and analytics)
10. Structured problem-solving approach (especially for strategy and consulting)
11. Going beyond the scope of the role
12. Decision with imperfect or incomplete information
Each story should be tagged with the categories it can answer.
 
### 1.3 Story coverage matrix
 
For each story, fill in:
 
| Story title | Categories it covers | Lane fit | Quantified result |
|---|---|---|---|
| Repackaged SKU pricing | 4 (cross-functional conflict), 6 (ambiguity), 9 (data-driven) | Strategy, PM, analytics | ARPU +14%, $X impact |
| Churn prediction model | 9 (data-driven), 10 (structured), 11 (beyond scope) | Analytics, PM | AUC 0.82, $480K ARR recovered |
 
Aim for at least one story per category. If a category has no story, that is a gap to identify and either build (find a real example from the candidate's history) or acknowledge (in the interview, "I do not have a textbook example of X, but the closest analog is...").
 
### 1.4 Lane-specific interview emphasis
 
**Strategy interviews emphasize:**
- Structured problem-solving (case interviews are core for consulting)
- Hypothesis-driven reasoning
- Executive stakeholder management
- Quantitative rigor
- Clarity of communication under pressure
- For consulting: market sizing, profitability cases, market entry cases, M&A cases. These are not behavioral stories; they require dedicated case-prep practice with case books and live partners.
**PM interviews emphasize:**
- Product sense (design a product, improve a product)
- Strategy (root-cause a metric drop, prioritize a roadmap)
- Execution (ship velocity, cross-functional leadership)
- Analytical (estimation, A/B test interpretation)
- Behavioral
- Many companies use distinct rubrics for each. Prepare separately.
**Analytics interviews emphasize:**
- SQL live coding or take-home
- Statistical reasoning (especially A/B testing)
- Case-style data interpretation
- Stakeholder communication (translate technical to business)
- Behavioral

## 2. The "tell me about yourself" answer
 
The most predictable opener. Most candidates blow it.
 
### 2.1 Structure
 
Roughly 90 seconds spoken. Four parts:
 
1. **Pre-MBA framing (20 to 30 seconds).** What you did before the MBA. Pick the throughline that supports your target lane. Do not chronologically recite every job; pick the strongest two or three experiences.
2. **MBA framing (20 to 30 seconds).** Why you went to business school. What you focused on. One or two specific experiences that signal lane fit (a strategy class project, a PM internship, an analytics fellowship).
3. **Post-MBA target (15 to 20 seconds).** What you are targeting and why. Specific enough that the interviewer can place you ("corporate strategy at a B2B SaaS company" not "something in business").
4. **Why this role and company (15 to 20 seconds).** Two specific reasons this role and company fit the path you described.
### 2.2 What kills this answer
 
- Chronological resume readback ("First I worked at...")
- Generic post-MBA target ("I'm interested in strategy roles")
- No throughline between pre-MBA, MBA, and target
- Filler ("Yeah, so, basically...")
- Length: under 60 seconds reads as unprepared; over 120 seconds reads as undisciplined
### 2.3 Template
 
> "Before business school, I spent [X years] in [function or industry], where I [specific accomplishment that ties to target lane]. The work I enjoyed most was [specific thing that bridges to target lane], which is why I came to [school] focused on [concentration or specific skills]. At [school] I [specific MBA experience that ties to target lane], and over the summer I [internship or project]. Post-MBA I am targeting [specific target lane and sub-segment]. I am particularly interested in [this company] because [one specific company fact plus one personal alignment reason]."
 
Practice it. Record yourself. Watch it back. Cut to 90 seconds.

## 3. LinkedIn About
 
The LinkedIn About section is high-leverage in 2026 because recruiters search and screen LinkedIn before opening resume attachments.
 
### 3.1 Length and structure
 
- 800 to 2,000 characters (LinkedIn limits to 2,600 but the first 250 are most important since LinkedIn truncates with "see more")
- Three to five short paragraphs
- First two sentences should hook the reader and signal the target lane
- Includes target-lane keywords from `target_lane_rubrics_and_bullets.md` integrated naturally
### 3.2 Structure template
 
**Paragraph 1: The hook (1 to 2 sentences)**
- Current focus: who you are, what you do, what you are targeting
- Read in isolation, this paragraph should let a recruiter searching for your target lane recognize you immediately
**Paragraph 2: The substance (3 to 5 sentences)**
- Two or three specific accomplishments with metrics
- Tie to target lane
- Show range: maybe one technical, one strategic, one cross-functional
**Paragraph 3: The throughline (2 to 4 sentences)**
- How your past, MBA, and target are connected
- Explicit framing of any pivot
- One or two values or motivators that humanize you without becoming generic
**Paragraph 4: The call to connect (1 to 2 sentences)**
- What conversations or opportunities you welcome
- Direct contact preference (email, LinkedIn message, calendar link)
### 3.3 What to avoid
 
- Third-person ("Josh is a strategic leader who..."). Use first person.
- Bullet point lists (LinkedIn About is prose; save bullets for Experience entries)
- Generic openers ("Passionate about driving impact...")
- Vague target lanes ("looking for opportunities in business strategy or product")
- Buzzword density without anchoring examples
### 3.4 Example structure (anonymized)
 
> Post-MBA decision scientist focused on growth and product analytics at consumer tech and media companies. Class of 2026 at [School], with five years of pre-MBA experience building data products at a streaming TV platform.
>
> Most recently, I led the analytics workstream behind a SKU repackage that lifted ARPU 14% across an $80M portfolio. Before that, I built a churn prediction model that recovered $480K in ARR and a measurement framework for paid acquisition that cut blended CAC 24%. I work fluently across SQL, Python, and the modern data stack (dbt, Snowflake, Looker), and I bring an MBA's view of how analytics ties to commercial decisions.
>
> The throughline across my career has been turning messy operational data into decisions executives can actually make. The MBA sharpened the strategic and financial framing; the pre-MBA work taught me how the modeling and infrastructure actually have to hold up. I'm now looking for analytics teams that take that combination seriously.
>
> If you are hiring for decision science, growth analytics, or analytics adjacent product roles, I'd welcome a conversation. Reach me at [email] or here on LinkedIn.

## 4. Networking outreach
 
### 4.1 The cold connection note
 
When sending a LinkedIn connection request without prior context.
 
- Keep it under 300 characters (LinkedIn limit is 300 for free accounts on connection requests; some users have higher limits)
- Specific reason for reaching out (mutual connection, alumni, company, function)
- Specific ask or no ask at all (sometimes the connection itself is the ask)
- No flattery
**Template:**
 
> Hi [First Name], I'm a [School] 2026 MBA targeting [lane] roles at [type of company]. I came across your background in [specific thing] and would value the chance to connect. Happy to keep the relationship informal and chat if useful.
 
### 4.2 The coffee chat request
 
After connection or with a warm intro.
 
- Three to five sentences
- Specific reason you are reaching out to this person specifically
- Explicit ask: 20 to 30 minutes, virtual or in person, your flexibility on time
- No attached documents in the first message
**Template:**
 
> Hi [First Name],
>
> Thanks for connecting. I'm a [School] 2026 MBA recruiting into [target lane] roles. I'm particularly interested in [their company] given [specific reason] and would love 20 minutes of your time to learn how you think about [a specific question relevant to their role or company].
>
> I have flexibility over the next two weeks. Would [specific date and time] or [specific date and time] work? I can also send a calendar link if easier.
>
> Thanks,
> [Name]
 
### 4.3 The follow-up after a conversation
 
Within 24 hours of the call. Three to five sentences.
 
- Thank them
- Reference one specific thing they said that stuck
- State what you are doing next based on the conversation (if anything)
- Leave the door open without being pushy
**Template:**
 
> Hi [First Name],
>
> Thanks for the time today. The point you made about [specific thing from the conversation] is the most useful framing I have heard on that question. I'm going to [concrete next action based on their advice], and I'll let you know how it goes.
>
> If a relevant opening crosses your desk, I'd appreciate the heads up. Thanks again.
 
### 4.4 The referral ask
 
Only after a meaningful conversation, only when the relationship justifies it. Do not ask for referrals from cold connections.
 
- Direct ask, no preamble
- Make it easy for them: send the job link, a one-line description, and your resume
- Acknowledge it is a real ask; do not pretend otherwise
**Template:**
 
> Hi [First Name],
>
> Following up on our conversation last [day or week]. The [specific role title] role on your team is open, and I'd like to apply. Would you be open to referring me, or to passing my resume to the hiring manager directly? Either would be valuable.
>
> Resume attached. Job link: [URL]. Happy to provide anything else helpful.
>
> Thanks for considering.

## 5. The pivot pitch
 
Candidates pivoting industries or functions need a 30-second answer for "Why are you switching?"
 
### 5.1 Structure
 
- One sentence: what you did before
- One sentence: what you learned that you are bringing forward
- One sentence: what you are pivoting toward and why specifically
- One sentence: why this is credible (the MBA, specific projects, specific skills)
### 5.2 What to avoid
 
- Bashing the prior industry or function
- Generic ambition ("I want broader impact")
- Vague target ("I'm exploring my options")
- Pretending there is no pivot when there obviously is
### 5.3 Example
 
> "I spent five years building data and analytics infrastructure at a streaming TV platform. The work I cared about most was using that data to inform commercial decisions, not just to report on them. That pulled me into business school. Post-MBA I'm targeting decision science and growth analytics roles where the analytics work is directly tied to revenue and product decisions. The MBA gave me the strategic and financial vocabulary; the pre-MBA experience gives me the technical credibility."
 
Practice this until it lands at 25 to 35 seconds and sounds natural rather than rehearsed.

## 6. Quick reference: interview prep workflow
 
1. List every resume bullet. Tag each with the interview categories it can support.
2. Identify gaps where no resume bullet supports a common category.
3. For each gap, mine memory for an example from work, MBA, or extracurriculars that fits.
4. Build a 8 to 12 story bank with CARL structure for each.
5. Practice the "tell me about yourself" answer separately; it is its own discipline.
6. For consulting and strategy: add case prep separately. The behavioral story bank is necessary but not sufficient.
7. For PM: add product sense, estimation, and execution prep separately.
8. For analytics: add SQL practice and statistical reasoning prep separately.
