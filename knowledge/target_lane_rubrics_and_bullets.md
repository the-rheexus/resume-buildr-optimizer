# Target Lane Rubrics and Bullet Library
 
Per-lane keywords, expected skills, recruiter heuristics, and worked weak-to-strong bullet rewrites for strategy, product management, and analytics.
 
---
 
## 1. How to use this file
 
When advising on a specific bullet, resume section, or lane positioning question:
 
1. Identify which of the three lanes the candidate is targeting for this specific application.
2. Match the candidate's content against the rubric for that lane.
3. Reference the bullet library for parallel examples.
4. If the candidate is targeting two lanes simultaneously (common for analytics and PM, or strategy and corporate strategy), show how the same bullet can be angled for each.

## 2. Strategy lane
 
### 2.1 What "strategy" covers
 
| Sub-lane | Typical employers |
|---|---|
| Strategy consulting (top tier) | McKinsey, BCG, Bain |
| Strategy consulting (Big 4 strategy arms) | Strategy&, Deloitte Monitor, EY-Parthenon, KPMG Strategy, Accenture Strategy |
| Strategy consulting (boutique) | LEK, Oliver Wyman, ZS, Kearney, Putnam, Trinity, Roland Berger, Simon-Kucher |
| Corporate strategy (F500) | Strategy teams at large enterprises, often a 2-to-3-year rotation followed by GM track |
| Strategy and Operations (tech) | Stripe BizOps, Airbnb S&O, Uber S&O, Google gTech, Meta XFN strategy |
| Internal strategy (mid-market) | Strategic planning, corporate development, M&A integration |
 
### 2.2 Recruiter heuristics for strategy
 
Recruiters and hiring managers look for:
 
1. **Structured thinking visible in bullet form.** Bullets that read as "problem to approach to outcome." MECE framing implicit even if not stated.
2. **Prestigious brand or rapid trajectory.** Top employer names, or visible promotions and scope expansion at lesser-known employers.
3. **Senior stakeholder exposure.** Bullets that name executives, boards, or C-suite presentations.
4. **Quantitative rigor.** Modeling, sizing, business cases. Hard numbers attached to recommendations.
5. **Cross-functional influence.** Leading without formal authority.
6. **Communication clarity.** The resume itself reads cleanly, which is taken as a proxy for client-readiness.
### 2.3 Strategy keywords and phrases
 
Use in context, not as a list:
 
- Hypothesis-driven analysis
- MECE structure (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive)
- Top-down and bottom-up modeling
- Market sizing
- Competitive analysis, competitive teardown
- Business case, investment case
- Three-year strategic plan
- Growth strategy, market entry strategy, exit strategy
- Portfolio strategy, capital allocation
- Pricing strategy, pricing model
- M&A, due diligence, post-merger integration
- Cost takeout, operating model redesign
- Executive communication, board presentation
- Stakeholder management
- Financial modeling, DCF, LBO (for finance-adjacent strategy)
- Strategic planning cycle
- Initiative prioritization
### 2.4 Strategy hard skills
 
- Financial modeling (Excel; intermediate to advanced)
- Market sizing (TAM, SAM, SOM frameworks)
- Competitive analysis frameworks (Porter, value chain, capability mapping)
- Slide deck design and storytelling (PowerPoint, Google Slides; visual hierarchy, executive-ready output)
- Quantitative analysis (Excel, SQL is a plus, Python or R is a strong signal for tech-adjacent strategy)
- AI-augmented analysis (Claude, ChatGPT, GPT-4 for research synthesis, document analysis, draft generation; cite specific tools and specific productivity outcomes)
### 2.5 Strategy disqualifiers in the resume scan
 
- Bullets that describe tasks ("created decks," "did research") without naming the strategic question or business outcome
- Buzzwords without specifics ("led strategic initiatives," "drove transformation")
- No senior stakeholder exposure visible anywhere
- No quantified business impact in any bullet
- Lane mismatch: bullets that read like project management or pure analytics with no strategic framing

## 3. Product Management lane
 
### 3.1 What "PM" covers
 
| Sub-lane | Typical employers |
|---|---|
| Big tech PM | Google, Meta, Amazon, Microsoft, Apple |
| B2B SaaS PM | Salesforce, ServiceNow, Snowflake, Stripe, Notion, Figma, Atlassian, HubSpot |
| Consumer PM | Airbnb, DoorDash, Uber, Spotify, Pinterest, Reddit |
| AI/ML PM | OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Microsoft AI, ML platform teams at any large tech company |
| Growth PM | Companies with a named growth function; often consumer or SMB SaaS |
| Platform and infrastructure PM | API products, developer tools, data platforms |
| APM and RPM programs | Google APM, Meta RPM, Microsoft PMA (often closed to recent MBAs but worth checking) |
 
### 3.2 Recruiter heuristics for PM
 
1. **Owned outcomes, not contributions.** "I built and shipped X that drove Y" beats "I worked on the team that built X."
2. **Zero-to-one and scale stories.** Building something new, or scaling something existing. Both are valuable; pick what matches the role.
3. **Technical fluency.** Not necessarily code; the ability to make tradeoffs with engineers, debate architecture choices, and reason about data.
4. **Customer obsession with evidence.** Specific customer research, specific insights, specific product changes.
5. **Cross-functional leadership.** Engineering, design, data science, sales, marketing, legal. Bullets should show influence across multiple functions.
6. **Metrics literacy.** Knows what to measure and why. North Star, input metrics, counter-metrics. LTV, CAC, retention curves, funnel.
### 3.3 PM keywords and phrases
 
- Product strategy
- Roadmap, product roadmap, quarterly planning
- User research, customer discovery, customer interviews
- Jobs-to-be-done (JTBD)
- MVP, prototype, alpha, beta
- A/B testing, experimentation, hypothesis testing
- Feature flag, controlled rollout
- OKRs, KPIs, North Star metric
- Activation, retention, engagement, monetization
- LTV, CAC, payback period, net retention
- Cross-functional team, engineering and design partnership
- Product-market fit
- Launch, GTM (go-to-market), positioning
- Roadmap prioritization (RICE, ICE, weighted scoring)
- Customer feedback loops
- Telemetry, instrumentation, product analytics
- For AI/ML PM: prompt engineering, evals, RAG, fine-tuning, agent workflows, LLM evaluation, hallucination mitigation, safety reviews
### 3.4 PM hard skills
 
- SQL (intermediate at minimum; can pull and join data without engineering support)
- Product analytics platforms (Amplitude, Mixpanel, Heap, Pendo)
- Experimentation platforms or frameworks (statsig, Eppo, internal tooling; A/B test design and statistical literacy)
- Design tools at a basic level (Figma read access, ability to sketch wireframes)
- Project tracking (Jira, Linear, Asana)
- Roadmap and documentation tools (Productboard, Notion, Confluence)
- Specification writing (PRDs)
- AI tools as part of the product workflow (Claude, ChatGPT for spec drafts, user research synthesis, competitive analysis)
### 3.5 PM disqualifiers in the resume scan
 
- All bullets are project management ("ran standups, tracked timelines") with no product judgment visible
- No metrics anywhere; PM bullets that name an outcome without a number
- No customer-facing work; no user research, no interviews, no feedback synthesis
- Lane mismatch: bullets that read like consulting (analysis without ownership) or analytics (data work without product decisions)
- Buzzword density ("agile transformation," "stakeholder alignment") without specific shipped product

## 4. Analytics lane
 
### 4.1 What "analytics" covers
 
| Sub-lane | Typical employers |
|---|---|
| Decision science | Meta, Airbnb, Stripe, Pinterest; embedded with product teams |
| Business intelligence | F500 BI teams, often inside Finance or Strategy |
| Marketing and growth analytics | DTC consumer brands, B2B SaaS marketing teams, agencies, ad-tech |
| Analytics consulting | ZS, Putnam Associates, Trinity, McKinsey QuantumBlack, BCG Gamma |
| Data product roles | Companies where data is the product (Snowflake, Databricks, dbt Labs, Looker) |
| Revenue operations or RevOps analytics | B2B SaaS RevOps and sales analytics |
 
### 4.2 Recruiter heuristics for analytics
 
1. **Technical depth visible in bullets.** Specific tools, languages, modeling approaches.
2. **Business impact translation.** Analytics work tied to a business decision or outcome, not just a number computed.
3. **Stakeholder management for non-technical executives.** Bullets that show working with marketing, product, finance, or leadership.
4. **Data infrastructure literacy.** Modern data stack tools (dbt, Snowflake, Databricks, Airflow) are increasingly expected for senior analytics roles.
5. **Statistical rigor.** A/B testing, causal inference, regression, cohort analysis.
6. **Communication.** Can a non-technical exec read your bullet and understand the impact?
### 4.3 Analytics keywords and phrases
 
- SQL (advanced)
- Python or R for analytics
- A/B testing, multi-armed bandit, sequential testing
- Experimentation design, power analysis, statistical significance
- Causal inference, difference-in-differences, regression discontinuity, propensity score matching
- Cohort analysis, retention curves, churn analysis
- LTV modeling, CAC analysis, payback period
- Attribution modeling, multi-touch attribution, marketing mix modeling
- Forecasting, demand forecasting, time-series analysis
- Segmentation, clustering, RFM analysis
- KPI definition, North Star metric
- ETL, ELT, data pipeline
- Modern data stack: dbt, Snowflake, BigQuery, Databricks, Redshift, Airflow, Fivetran
- Dashboarding: Tableau, Looker, Looker Studio, Power BI, Amazon QuickSight, Mode, Hex, Sigma
- Data quality, data governance
### 4.4 Analytics hard skills
 
- SQL (advanced; window functions, CTEs, complex joins, query optimization)
- Python (data manipulation with pandas, statistical analysis with scipy or statsmodels, basic ML with scikit-learn)
- R (if relevant; some analytics functions still prefer R for statistical rigor)
- Statistical methods (hypothesis testing, regression, causal inference)
- Experimentation (design, analysis, interpretation)
- Data visualization tools (Tableau, Looker, or equivalent)
- Modern data stack (dbt, Snowflake or Databricks, Airflow or similar orchestration)
- For AI-adjacent analytics: LLM integration, embeddings, RAG for analytics, AI-augmented data exploration
### 4.5 Analytics disqualifiers in the resume scan
 
- Bullets that describe the data computed without naming the decision or outcome
- Tool name-dropping without context: "Used SQL, Python, Tableau" in a list with no demonstrated use
- No stakeholder management visible
- Pure technical bullets with no business framing for a business-facing analytics role
- Mismatched seniority: junior bullets ("supported team in analyzing data") for a senior analytics role

## 5. Bullet library: weak to strong rewrites
 
Each example shows a weak bullet, the rewrite, and a one-line note on what the rewrite optimizes for.
 
### 5.1 Strategy bullets
 
**Example A: Strategic project work**
 
- Weak: Helped manage strategic projects for senior leadership.
- Strong: Led 5-person cross-functional team analyzing entry strategy for a $2B adjacent market; built bottom-up demand model and recommended $40M phased entry path; recommendation adopted by CEO and launched in Q4 2024.
- Optimizes for: scope, scale, stakeholder seniority, business decision adopted.
**Example B: Competitive analysis**
 
- Weak: Conducted competitive analysis to inform pricing decisions.
- Strong: Built MECE competitive teardown across 12 CTV ad-tech competitors; identified 3 pricing gaps and 2 product gaps; recommendations drove pricing repackage that lifted ARPU 18% over two quarters.
- Optimizes for: structured framework named, specific findings, quantified business outcome.
**Example C: Business case**
 
- Weak: Created business case for new initiative.
- Strong: Authored investment case for $12M data infrastructure overhaul; modeled three-year cost-benefit with sensitivity analysis on adoption, presented to CFO and board, secured full funding in single meeting.
- Optimizes for: ownership ("authored"), specific deliverable, executive audience, decision outcome.
**Example D: Cross-functional alignment**
 
- Weak: Worked with multiple stakeholders to align on strategy.
- Strong: Aligned product, engineering, and sales leadership on revised go-to-market for SMB segment; negotiated tradeoffs across competing roadmap priorities, resulting in committed quarterly launch plan covering 4 product lines.
- Optimizes for: named functions, negotiation language, concrete artifact (launch plan).
### 5.2 Product Management bullets
 
**Example A: Feature ownership**
 
- Weak: Owned product roadmap for a key feature.
- Strong: Owned roadmap for video player feature serving 4M monthly viewers; ran 11 A/B tests over 6 months that lifted completion rate from 62% to 71% and added $1.2M ARR through reduced ad-load skip.
- Optimizes for: scale (users), specific experimentation, before-and-after metric, dollar impact.
**Example B: Cross-functional team leadership**
 
- Weak: Worked with engineering and design to ship features.
- Strong: Led cross-functional team of 4 engineers, 2 designers, and 1 data scientist to ship recommendation engine v2; launch experiment showed 14% lift in session length and 9% lift in 30-day retention.
- Optimizes for: explicit team composition, named outputs, two-dimensional impact.
**Example C: Customer research**
 
- Weak: Conducted user research to inform product decisions.
- Strong: Ran 22 user interviews and 3 surveys (n=480) to validate a new onboarding hypothesis; insights drove redesign that lifted day-7 activation from 38% to 51%.
- Optimizes for: research volume, specific instrument types, before-and-after activation lift.
**Example D: AI/ML product work**
 
- Weak: Worked on AI features.
- Strong: Defined product specs and evals for LLM-powered customer support feature serving 30K monthly tickets; collaborated with ML team on prompt engineering and RAG retrieval, reducing hallucination rate from 12% to 3.5% and lifting CSAT 8 points.
- Optimizes for: AI/ML PM-specific vocabulary (specs, evals, RAG, hallucination), business outcome (CSAT).
### 5.3 Analytics bullets
 
**Example A: Ad delivery and attribution integrity**
 
- Weak: Maintained dashboards tracking ad delivery for the operations team.
- Strong: Audited attribution integrity across the third-party tracking pipeline feeding programmatic and CTV campaign reporting; cross-validated viewer geography and device signals against raw delivery logs in QuickSight, surfaced mismapping across active campaigns, and corrected pipeline routing to protect advertiser-facing reporting accuracy.
- Optimizes for: pipeline integrity is invisible work; the rewrite frames it through advertiser-facing consequence, names the discovery method (cross-validation against raw logs), and the remediation step.
**Example B: Audience analytics with syndicated data**
 
- Weak: Analyzed audience data to support ad sales.
- Strong: Built audience analyses across mobile carrier and lifestyle segments using third-party syndicated data (MRI-Simmons); translated demographic and behavioral signals into segment-level recommendations adopted by programming and ad sales leadership for inventory and pricing decisions.
- Optimizes for: third-party data source named (industry-standard tool signal), translation work between technical and commercial, recommendations adopted by named senior functions.
**Example C: Experimentation**
 
- Weak: Ran A/B tests for marketing.
- Strong: Designed and analyzed 18 paid acquisition experiments across 3 channels; identified two winning creative variants that improved blended CAC by 24% and shifted $2.1M in annual spend toward higher-LTV cohorts.
- Optimizes for: experiment volume, channel breadth, cost outcome, strategic budget reallocation.
**Example D: Stakeholder management**
 
- Weak: Presented findings to leadership.
- Strong: Synthesized 3 months of cohort and attribution analysis into 12-slide executive readout for CMO and CFO; recommendation to consolidate spend across two channels was adopted and saved $890K in Q4 ad budget.
- Optimizes for: synthesis (not just analysis), audience specificity, recommendation adopted, dollar outcome.
### 5.4 Multi-lane bullets (angle the same work for two lanes)
 
Same underlying work, three different bullets depending on target lane.
 
**The work:** Led an internal pricing analysis that recommended a SKU repackage.
 
- Strategy angle: Led pricing strategy analysis across 4 product SKUs in $80M portfolio; built bottom-up willingness-to-pay model, presented repackage recommendation to executive committee, repackage launched and lifted ARPU 14% in two quarters.
- PM angle: Owned SKU repackage as cross-functional PM lead; collaborated with sales, finance, and engineering to redesign packaging and pricing pages; launched in 6 weeks and lifted self-serve conversion 22% with no change to ARR per customer.
- Analytics angle: Built willingness-to-pay model in Python using customer survey data (n=1,200) and historical billing data; quantified price elasticity across 4 SKUs and 3 customer segments; analysis informed repackage that lifted ARPU 14% with negligible churn impact.
The underlying facts are the same. The framing, verb choice, and emphasis change.

## 6. Quick reference: bullet quality test
 
Before accepting any bullet on the resume, it should pass all of these:
 
1. Does it start with a strong action verb? (not "responsible for," "helped," "worked on")
2. Does the reader know what problem or context the work addressed?
3. Does the bullet name a specific action the candidate took?
4. Is there a quantified business outcome or, at minimum, a clear scope and scale?
5. Does the language match the target lane (see sections 2.3, 3.3, 4.3)?
6. Could the candidate defend this bullet in detail under interview pressure?
If any answer is no, rewrite.
